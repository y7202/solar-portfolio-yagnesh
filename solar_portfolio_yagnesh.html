Executive Summary

The Solar-Powered Water Filtration System is a community-focused innovation that combines photovoltaic energy and multi-stage filtration to deliver safe drinking water in off-grid and energy-constrained regions. This portfolio documents the problem definition, hypothesis testing, MVP development, user feedback, SWOT analysis, and my decision-making improvement journey.

Market & Problem

More than 2.2 billion people worldwide lack safely managed drinking water. In many regions, power availability is low or unreliable, making electric or fuel-based filtration impractical. This product targets rural households, community centers, NGOs, and schools that need reliable, low-cost clean water solutions.

Customer Jobs • Pains • Gains

Jobs: Access potable water, reduce monthly water spending, ensure safety for children and elderly.

Pains: Contaminated sources, unreliable grids, high operation costs.

Expected Gains: Dependable supply, health improvement, lower long-term cost.

Solution & MVP

The MVP integrates a 150W solar array, a modular multi-stage filtration stack (sediment -> activated carbon -> UV -> polishing membrane), optional small battery storage, and a simple LED status panel. The design prioritizes durability, low maintenance, and local serviceability.

Key MVP Components

  • Solar array (150W nominal) with regulator
  • Multi-stage cartridge filters (field-replaceable)
  • UV disinfection module
  • 12V backup battery option
  • Simple indicators and maintenance guide

Product/Market Fit Experiment

The experiment tested adoption, technical reliability, and pricing sensitivity. Key outcomes informed an iteration strategy.

Key Outcomes

  • Water clarity and pathogen reduction met WHO thresholds in pilot tests.
  • 87% of households indicated willingness to adopt at ≤ $250/unit (community pricing).
  • Main barriers: long-term durability, filter supply logistics, and initial cost.

Decision: Iterate

Rather than pivot, the evidence supports iterating the MVP. Planned iterations include stronger casings, cartridge redesign, small local assembly, and a battery-backed regulator for cloudy conditions.

SWOT Analysis

Strengths

Renewable, measurable health impact, modular design, strong pilot interest.

Weaknesses

Upfront cost, supply-chain for cartridges, sunlight variability.

Opportunities

NGO partnerships, local manufacturing, school and clinic pilots.

Threats

Cheaper non-renewable filters, funding cuts, regulatory hurdles.
